What's actually at stake

A governor isn't one vote among 100 — they run the state. Here's what the winner controls.

The budget
Proposes the ~$12B state budget — schools, Medicaid, and housing funds all start here.
The veto pen
Both chambers of Nevada's Legislature are Democratic. The governor decides what becomes law.
The Guard & ICE
Commands the National Guard and sets how far state agencies cooperate with federal immigration enforcement.
2030 redistricting
The winner will hold the veto pen when Nevada redraws its congressional maps after the 2030 census.

The backstory

Nevada does this every time: the 2022 governor's race was decided by about 15,000 votes, and this one is polling inside a single point. Lombardo has governed as a Republican check on a Democratic legislature — signing a record teacher-raise fund and a $183M housing program, while vetoing 162 bills on rent caps, paid family leave, guns, and school meals.

Ford's case is cost of living: Nevada has had the nation's highest unemployment rate for over a year, housing costs are at record highs, and he blames the "Lombardo-Trump economy." Lombardo's case is his record: job growth, a $14M war chest, and a warning that a Democratic governor plus a Democratic legislature would leave no one to say no in Carson City.

The deciders: Latino voters (Ford led by 24 points in spring polling), the Washoe County suburbs, and an unusually large undecided bloc. Trump won Nevada in 2024 — but he's underwater here now, and both campaigns know it.
